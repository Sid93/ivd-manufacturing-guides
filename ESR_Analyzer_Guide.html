1 What is ESR Testing?

▾

The Basics

ESRErythrocyte Sedimentation Rate — the speed at which red blood cells settle to the bottom of a standardized tube in one hour, measured in mm/hr. stands for Erythrocyte Sedimentation Rate. It is one of the oldest and simplest laboratory tests still in wide clinical use. The test measures how fast RBCsRed Blood Cells (erythrocytes) — the most abundant cells in blood, responsible for carrying oxygen. They are biconcave discs about 7 micrometers in diameter. (red blood cells) settle to the bottom of a standardized glass tube under the influence of gravity over one hour. The result is reported in millimeters per hour (mm/hr).

Historical Background

The phenomenon of erythrocyte sedimentation was first described scientifically by Edmund Biernacki in 1897 in Poland, who noted that blood from sick patients settled faster than blood from healthy individuals. The test was later refined and standardized by Alf Westergren in 1921, who established the vertical tube method that became the reference standard worldwide. The Westergren method uses a 200mm tall tube filled with anticoagulated blood diluted with sodium citrate, and results are read after exactly 60 minutes.

Why Is ESR Clinically Important?

ESR is a non-specific marker of inflammation. When the body experiences inflammation from any cause, certain proteins in the blood (particularly fibrinogenA plasma protein produced by the liver that plays a key role in blood clotting and also promotes red blood cell aggregation, which increases the ESR. and immunoglobulinsAntibody proteins produced by plasma cells of the immune system. Elevated levels promote RBC aggregation and increase ESR.) increase in concentration. These proteins cause red blood cells to stick together in stacks called rouleauxRouleaux (singular: rouleau) — stacks of red blood cells that form because of increased plasma protein concentrations. They resemble a stack of coins and settle faster than individual RBCs., which settle faster due to their greater mass.

ESR is elevated in many conditions:

  • Infections — bacterial, viral, fungal, and parasitic
  • Autoimmune diseases — rheumatoid arthritis, systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE), polymyalgia rheumatica, temporal arteritis
  • Cancers — especially multiple myeloma, lymphoma, and metastatic cancers
  • Chronic kidney disease and nephrotic syndrome
  • Tissue injury — post-surgery, trauma, or myocardial infarction
  • Pregnancy — physiologically elevated

Clinical Workflow

ESR is typically ordered as part of an inflammatory disease workup. A doctor may order ESR when a patient presents with unexplained fever, joint pain, weight loss, or other signs of systemic inflammation. The result helps the clinician decide whether further investigations are needed and can be used to monitor disease activity and treatment response over time. In conditions like temporal arteritis and polymyalgia rheumatica, ESR is a primary diagnostic criterion.

Why Photometric Rheology?

Photometric rheology is the clear winner for a modern, high-throughput laboratory. It offers the fastest results (20 seconds versus 60 minutes for Westergren), requires the smallest sample volume (100–150 μL versus 1.6 mL), and eliminates biohazard risk since the sample is handled within a closed fluidic system. The method has been clinically validated to correlate well with the Westergren reference method (r > 0.95 in published studies). Leading global brands like Alifax (Italy), Alcor Scientific (USA), and Diesse (Italy) all use variations of this technology.

3 How Photometric Rheology Works

▾

Core Principle

Photometric rheology measures RBC aggregationThe process by which red blood cells clump together forming rouleaux. The rate and extent of aggregation is directly related to ESR — more aggregation means higher ESR. kinetics using infrared light. An IR LEDInfrared Light-Emitting Diode — emits light at 880nm wavelength, which is well-absorbed by hemoglobin in red blood cells, making it ideal for optical blood measurements. emitting at 880nm wavelength shines through a thin layer of blood held in a quartz flow cellA small transparent chamber (typically 10×2×0.8mm cavity) made of optical-grade quartz through which blood flows and light passes for measurement.. A photodiodeA semiconductor device that converts light into electrical current. The current is proportional to the intensity of light hitting the sensor. on the other side detects how much light passes through.

When blood is first introduced into the flow cell, individual red blood cells scatter light, resulting in lower transmitted light (higher optical densityOptical Density (OD) — a logarithmic measure of how much light is absorbed or scattered by a sample. OD = -log₁₀(Transmitted / Incident light intensity).). As RBCs begin to aggregate into rouleaux, the gaps between the stacks allow more light through, and the optical density decreases. The rate of this optical density change directly correlates with the ESR value.

10-Step Measurement Workflow

The complete measurement cycle from sample insertion to ESR result takes approximately 45 seconds, with the actual optical measurement requiring only 20 seconds:

1
Sample Loading: The operator places an EDTA blood collection tube into the tube rack on the analyzer. The rack can hold 10–20 tubes depending on the model configuration.
2
Barcode Scanning: A barcode reader automatically scans the label on the tube to capture the patient ID and sample details, linking the result to the correct patient in the Laboratory Information System (LISLaboratory Information System — software that manages laboratory data including patient information, test orders, results, and quality control.).
3
Probe Descent: The aspiration probe (a thin stainless steel needle) descends vertically into the blood tube. A capacitive liquid level sensorA sensor that detects the presence of liquid by measuring changes in electrical capacitance when the probe tip contacts the blood surface. on the probe tip detects when it reaches the blood surface, preventing air aspiration.
4
Sample Aspiration: A peristaltic pumpA pump that moves fluid through flexible tubing by compressing the tube with rotating rollers. The fluid never contacts the pump mechanism, maintaining sterility. draws approximately 150 μL of blood through the aspiration probe and tubing toward the flow cell. The pump uses silicone tubing and provides precise, pulsation-free flow.
5
Flow Cell Filling: Blood fills the quartz flow cell, which has an internal cavity of approximately 10mm × 2mm × 0.8mm. The cell is made of optical-grade quartz for maximum IR transparency. An optical sensor detects when the cell is properly filled.
6
Temperature Stabilization: A Peltier moduleA thermoelectric device (TEC) that can both heat and cool by passing current through it. Used to maintain the flow cell at exactly 25°C regardless of ambient temperature. maintains the flow cell at exactly 25.0°C ± 0.1°C. Temperature affects blood viscosity and RBC aggregation kinetics, so precise control is essential for reproducible results. An NTC thermistor provides feedback for a PID control loop.
7
Optical Illumination: The IR LED (880nm) illuminates the flow cell, and the photodiode on the opposite side begins measuring transmitted light intensity. The LED current is held constant at 100mA using an LM317-based constant current source for stable optical output.
8
Data Acquisition: Over a 20-second measurement window, the ADCAnalog-to-Digital Converter — converts the continuous analog voltage from the photodiode circuit into discrete digital numbers that the microcontroller can process. The 12-bit ADC provides 4096 levels of resolution. samples the photodiode signal at 1 kHz, collecting 20,000 data points. The raw signal passes through a transimpedance amplifierTIA — an electronic circuit that converts the tiny current output of the photodiode (nanoamps) into a measurable voltage. The OPA128 is a precision electrometer-grade op-amp ideal for this purpose. (OPA128) and buffer stage (OPA2134) before digitization.
9
Curve Fitting: The firmware applies an exponential decay model to the optical density vs. time data using Levenberg-MarquardtA numerical optimization algorithm that finds the best-fit parameters for a nonlinear equation by iteratively minimizing the sum of squared residuals between the model and the data. nonlinear least-squares fitting. The model equation is:
OD(t) = OD₀ + dOD × (1 − exp(−t / τ))
where OD₀ is the initial optical density, dOD is the total change in optical density, and τ (tau) is the time constant of aggregation.
10
ESR Calculation: The fitted parameters (τ and dOD) are plugged into a calibration equation to compute the Westergren-equivalent ESR value:
ESR = a × τ + b × dOD + c
The calibration constants (a, b, c) are determined during manufacturing by running a set of 20 reference samples with known Westergren ESR values and performing linear regression. The result is displayed on screen, stored in memory, and transmitted to the LIS.

Main Controller

The brain of the analyzer is an STM32F407VGT6A 32-bit ARM Cortex-M4 microcontroller from STMicroelectronics with 168MHz clock, 1MB Flash, 192KB RAM, floating-point unit, and extensive peripherals. Widely used in industrial and medical devices. microcontroller — an ARM Cortex-M4 running at 168 MHz with a hardware floating-point unit (essential for real-time curve fitting). It features 1MB of Flash memory for firmware and 192KB of SRAM for runtime data. The MCU interfaces with all subsystems through its rich set of peripherals: SPI, I2C, UART, ADC, timers, and GPIO.

PCB Design

The main PCB is a 4-layer board measuring 200mm x 150mm with an ENIGElectroless Nickel Immersion Gold — a PCB surface finish that provides flat, solderable pads with excellent corrosion resistance. More expensive than HASL but better for fine-pitch components. (Electroless Nickel Immersion Gold) surface finish. The 4-layer stackup provides dedicated power and ground planes for noise isolation, which is critical for the sensitive optical measurement circuit. The board is designed in KiCad and manufactured by Indian PCB vendors with IPC Class 2 compliance.

Optical Signal Chain

The optical measurement electronics are the most critical part of the design:

  • LED Driver: An LM317 regulator configured as a constant-current source delivers exactly 100mA to the 880nm IR LED. Current regulation (rather than voltage) ensures stable optical output regardless of LED forward voltage drift with temperature.
  • Transimpedance Amplifier: An OPA128 electrometer-grade op-amp converts the photodiode's photocurrent (typically 10–500 nA) to voltage. The feedback resistor (10 MΩ) provides a gain of 10 mV/nA with ultra-low bias current (<75 fA).
  • Buffer Stage: An OPA2134 dual op-amp provides buffering and additional gain, with a low-pass filter (fc = 500 Hz) to reject high-frequency noise before the ADC.
  • ADC: The STM32's internal 12-bit ADC (4096 levels) samples at 1 kHz. For a typical optical signal range of 0–3.3V, this provides a resolution of ~0.8 mV per LSB, sufficient for the measurement.

Signal Processing Pipeline

The optical measurement data goes through a multi-stage processing pipeline:

  1. ADC Sampling: Raw 12-bit ADC values captured at 1 kHz (20,000 samples over 20 seconds)
  2. Optical Density Conversion: Raw ADC values are converted to optical density using: OD = −log₁₀(V_sample / V_reference)
  3. Moving Average Filter: A 10-point moving average smooths the OD signal, reducing random noise while preserving the exponential decay shape
  4. Levenberg-Marquardt Fitting: The smoothed OD(t) curve is fit to the exponential model: OD(t) = OD₀ + dOD × (1 − exp(−t/τ)). The algorithm iterates (typically 15–30 iterations) until convergence (χ² change < 0.001%)
  5. ESR Calculation: Fitted parameters are applied to the calibration equation: ESR = a × τ + b × dOD + c
  6. Result Validation: Checks for: measurement quality (R² > 0.95), result range (0–140 mm/hr), temperature within spec, and error flags

State Machine

The main control task implements a state machine with these states:

IDLE → SAMPLE_LOADING → ASPIRATING → MEASURING → RESULT → CLEANING → back to IDLE

Each state transition is guarded by preconditions (e.g., temperature must be stable before entering MEASURING). Any error condition (flow cell empty, motor stall, temperature out of range) triggers an immediate transition to an ERROR state with an appropriate alert on the display.

8 Step-by-Step Manufacturing Process

▾

Production Overview

Manufacturing the ESR Analyzer involves 9 major stages. At a target production volume of 500–1,000 units per year, a batch size of 10–20 units is optimal, balancing efficiency with inventory management. Total manufacturing labor cost is approximately ₹14,650 per unit (includes assembly, testing, calibration, and packaging).

Stage 1: PCB Fabrication and Assembly

For initial production, outsourced SMT assembly is recommended to ensure quality and avoid the high capital cost of pick-and-place equipment:

  • PCB bare boards fabricated per Gerber files — 4-layer, ENIG, IPC Class 2
  • Bill of Materials and component reels provided to the contract manufacturer
  • SMT assembly with solder paste stencil, reflow oven profile, and AOI (Automated Optical Inspection)
  • Through-hole components (connectors, power components) soldered by hand or wave
  • In-Circuit Test (ICT) or Flying Probe test for each board
  • Functional test: MCU boots, all peripherals respond, ADC reads reference voltage correctly

Stage 2: Mechanical Component Fabrication

  • Sheet metal (chassis): Laser cut, brake form, weld, powder coat. Outsource to local sheet metal vendor.
  • Injection molding: Tube rack, probe guide, cosmetic trim pieces. Outsource to plastics vendor. Tooling cost ~₹3–5 lakh per mold (one-time).
  • CNC machining: Optical bench from Aluminum 6061-T6 billet. Tight tolerance work — outsource to a precision CNC shop. Inspect with CMM (Coordinate Measuring Machine).
  • Incoming inspection: Dimensional checks on all mechanical parts per engineering drawings.

Stage 3: Optical Bench Assembly (7 Steps)

  1. Clean optical bench with isopropyl alcohol in a dust-free area
  2. Install IR LED in its precision bore with thermal compound, connect leads
  3. Install collimating lens in V-groove with optical adhesive (UV-cure)
  4. Install quartz flow cell in the holder bracket, apply RTV silicone seal around fluid ports
  5. Install collecting lens and bandpass filter in their respective mounts
  6. Install photodiode in its precision bore, connect to TIA circuit
  7. Alignment verification: Using a laser alignment tool, verify that all optical components are collinear within ±0.1mm. Measure reference optical density using a neutral density filter — must match expected value within ±2%.

Stage 4: Fluidic System Assembly (6 Steps)

  1. Assemble polypropylene manifold with O-ring seals on all ports
  2. Install 6-port rotary valve and connect to stepper motor actuator
  3. Mount peristaltic pump, install silicone tubing cassette
  4. Connect all tubing runs: aspiration probe → manifold → flow cell → waste
  5. Install DI water reservoir and waste container with level sensors
  6. Leak test: Pressurize the fluidic system to 1.5× operating pressure (approximately 1 bar) with DI water. Inspect all joints for leaks over 10 minutes. Zero leakage permitted.

Stage 5: Final Mechanical Integration

Mount the optical bench assembly, fluidic system, tube rack mechanism, and sample handling motors into the main chassis. Install linear rails, GT2 belt system, and Z-axis leadscrew. Verify smooth motion on both axes — no binding, no excessive play. Install vibration isolation grommets between optical bench and chassis.

Stage 6: Electronics Integration and Wiring (9 Steps)

  1. Mount main PCB on standoffs inside chassis
  2. Install Mean Well power supply and wire AC inlet with appropriate fusing
  3. Connect motor cables (4-pin) to A4988 drivers
  4. Connect optical signal cable (shielded) from photodiode circuit to main PCB
  5. Connect temperature sensor (thermistor) and Peltier power leads
  6. Connect Hall-effect sensors and capacitive liquid level sensor
  7. Mount and connect 5" TFT LCD display and touch panel
  8. Connect USB, Ethernet, and RS-232 ports to rear panel connectors
  9. Wiring inspection: Visual check of all connections, verify correct polarity, continuity test on critical power lines, and insulation resistance test at 500V DC (must be > 10 MΩ).

Stage 7: Firmware Flashing and Configuration

Flash the FreeRTOS firmware via the SWD (Serial Wire Debug) programming header using an ST-Link programmer. Set the serial number, manufacture date, and hardware revision in the configuration area of Flash. Run the built-in self-test (BIST) routine, which checks all peripherals, motors, sensors, display, and communication ports. The BIST takes approximately 3 minutes and provides a pass/fail report.

Stage 8: Optical Calibration (6-Step Procedure)

  1. Run 20 blood samples with known Westergren ESR values (covering the range 2–120 mm/hr)
  2. For each sample, record the photometric parameters: τ (time constant) and dOD (optical density change)
  3. Perform linear regression: ESR = a × τ + b × dOD + c to determine calibration constants
  4. Verify: R² must be > 0.95 and all residuals must be within ±15% or ±5 mm/hr (whichever is greater)
  5. Store calibration constants in protected Flash memory area (CRC-protected)
  6. Run 5 additional validation samples (not used in calibration) to confirm accuracy — all must be within ±10% of Westergren reference

Stage 9: Quality Control and Validation

Each unit undergoes a comprehensive QC protocol before shipment:

  • Precision test: Run the same sample 20 times — CV must be < 5%
  • Linearity test: 5 samples across the clinical range — R² > 0.99
  • Carryover test: High sample followed by low sample — carryover < 1%
  • Temperature stress: Operate at 15°C, 25°C, and 35°C ambient — results must agree within ±5%
  • Electrical safety: Earth continuity, insulation resistance, leakage current per IEC 60601-1
  • Burn-in: 48-hour continuous operation to detect early failures
  • Documentation: Generate test certificate, calibration certificate, and QC report
Production capacity: With a team of 4–6 technicians, this process yields 10–20 units per batch with a batch cycle time of 3–4 weeks. Annual capacity: 500–1,000 units. Labor cost: approximately ₹14,650 per unit.

Electrical Safety Standards

  • IEC 60601-1: General requirements for basic safety and essential performance of medical electrical equipment. Covers insulation, grounding, leakage current, mechanical strength, and temperature limits.
  • IEC 60601-1-2: Electromagnetic compatibility (EMC) requirements. The analyzer must not emit excessive electromagnetic interference and must be immune to external interference. Tests include radiated emissions, conducted emissions, electrostatic discharge, and surge immunity.

CDSCO Registration (Indian Regulatory)

The ESR Analyzer is classified as a Class C medical device by CDSCOCentral Drugs Standard Control Organisation — the Indian regulatory body for medical devices, equivalent to the US FDA. CDSCO classifies devices into Classes A–D based on risk level and grants manufacturing/import licenses. (Central Drugs Standard Control Organisation). The registration process involves:

1
Pre-submission: Prepare a Device Master File (DMF) containing: device description, intended use, design specifications, risk analysis, biocompatibility data (for blood-contacting components), software validation, performance data, and labeling.
2
Testing: Get the device tested at a BISBureau of Indian Standards — the national standards body of India. BIS-recognized labs perform the IEC 60601 safety and EMC testing required for CDSCO registration.-recognized laboratory for IEC 60601-1 (safety) and IEC 60601-1-2 (EMC). This typically costs ₹5–8 lakh and takes 3–6 months.
3
Application: Submit Form MD-14 to CDSCO with the DMF, test reports, QMS certificate (ISO 13485), clinical evidence, and prescribed fees.
4
Review: CDSCO reviews the application, may request additional data or site inspection. A Subject Expert Committee (SEC) evaluates the technical dossier.
5
Approval: Upon satisfactory review, CDSCO grants a manufacturing license. The entire process typically takes 17–33 months.

Validation Protocols

Before commercial release, three validation stages must be completed:

  • IQ (Installation Qualification): Verifies that the analyzer is properly installed per specifications — correct power supply, environmental conditions, all accessories present.
  • OQ (Operational Qualification): Verifies that the analyzer operates correctly under all specified conditions — temperature range, sample types, precision, accuracy, and error handling.
  • PQ (Performance Qualification): Verifies that the analyzer performs consistently over time under actual-use conditions in a clinical laboratory setting.

12 Glossary

▾
ESR (Erythrocyte Sedimentation Rate)
The rate at which red blood cells settle in a standardized tube over one hour, measured in mm/hr. A non-specific marker of inflammation.
Westergren Method
The gold-standard reference method for ESR measurement, using a 200mm vertical glass tube with citrated blood, read after 60 minutes. Established by Alf Westergren in 1921.
Photometric Rheology
A technique that measures ESR by shining infrared light through blood and analyzing the rate of optical density change as red blood cells aggregate. Produces results in ~20 seconds.
Rouleaux Formation
The stacking of red blood cells into coin-like columns caused by elevated plasma proteins (fibrinogen, immunoglobulins). Rouleaux settle faster than individual RBCs, increasing ESR.
Optical Density (OD)
A logarithmic measure of light absorption/scattering by a sample: OD = -log10(Transmitted/Incident intensity). Higher OD means less light passes through.
Transimpedance Amplifier (TIA)
An electronic circuit that converts the tiny photocurrent from a photodiode (nanoamps) into a measurable voltage signal, using a high-value feedback resistor.
Peltier Module (TEC)
A thermoelectric cooler/heater that transfers heat from one side to the other when current flows through it. Used to maintain the flow cell at a precise temperature (25°C).
FreeRTOS
A popular open-source Real-Time Operating System for microcontrollers that provides task scheduling, inter-task communication, and timing services. Used in many medical devices.
Levenberg-Marquardt Algorithm
A numerical optimization method for fitting nonlinear equations to data. It combines gradient descent and Gauss-Newton approaches to find the best-fit parameters. Used here for exponential curve fitting.
CDSCO
Central Drugs Standard Control Organisation — India's national regulatory authority for medical devices and pharmaceuticals, responsible for device classification, registration, and market surveillance.
ISO 13485
The international quality management system standard specific to medical devices. Covers design controls, risk management, production controls, traceability, and post-market surveillance.
CLSI
Clinical and Laboratory Standards Institute — a global organization that develops consensus standards for laboratory testing methods, including precision, accuracy, linearity, and method comparison protocols.
HL7
Health Level Seven — an international standard for the electronic exchange of clinical and administrative data between healthcare information systems. HL7 v2.x uses pipe-delimited text messages.
NABL
National Accreditation Board for Testing and Calibration Laboratories — the Indian accreditation body that certifies testing and calibration laboratories per ISO/IEC 17025 and ISO 15189.
BIS
Bureau of Indian Standards — India's national standards body that publishes Indian Standards (IS) and recognizes testing laboratories for product certification and regulatory compliance.
COGS
Cost of Goods Sold — the total direct cost of manufacturing a product, including materials, direct labor, and allocated manufacturing overhead.
AMC (Annual Maintenance Contract)
A service agreement where the customer pays an annual fee for preventive maintenance, breakdown repairs, and consumable supply. AMC typically costs 8-12% of the instrument price per year.
CER (Clinical Evaluation Report)
A document that compiles and analyzes clinical data to demonstrate that a medical device is safe and performs as intended. Required for regulatory submissions.
DMF (Device Master File)
A comprehensive file containing all design, manufacturing, and quality information about a medical device. Submitted to regulatory authorities as part of the registration process.
QMS (Quality Management System)
A formalized system of policies, processes, and procedures for ensuring that products and services consistently meet customer and regulatory requirements.
IQC (Incoming Quality Control)
Inspection and testing of purchased raw materials and components before they enter the manufacturing process, to verify compliance with specifications.
OQC (Outgoing Quality Control)
Final inspection and testing of finished products before shipment, verifying that all specifications, safety requirements, and cosmetic standards are met.
IPQC (In-Process Quality Control)
Quality checks performed at critical stages during the manufacturing process to catch defects early and prevent them from propagating to later stages.
CRP (C-Reactive Protein)
An acute-phase protein measured in blood that rises rapidly during inflammation. Often ordered alongside ESR as a more specific and faster-responding inflammatory marker.
CBC (Complete Blood Count)
A comprehensive blood test measuring red blood cells, white blood cells, hemoglobin, hematocrit, and platelets. One of the most commonly ordered laboratory tests worldwide.
EDTA
Ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid — an anticoagulant used in blood collection tubes (purple/lavender top). EDTA chelates calcium ions to prevent blood clotting. Standard for ESR and CBC testing.
LIS (Laboratory Information System)
Software that manages all aspects of laboratory operations including test ordering, sample tracking, result reporting, quality control, and billing. Analyzers connect to LIS via HL7 or serial protocols.
PID Control
Proportional-Integral-Derivative control — a feedback control algorithm that continuously adjusts an output (e.g., Peltier current) to minimize the error between a measured value and a setpoint (e.g., 25°C).
STM32
A family of 32-bit ARM Cortex-M microcontrollers made by STMicroelectronics. Widely used in industrial and medical applications for their performance, peripherals, and ecosystem support.
IVD (In Vitro Diagnostics)
Medical devices and reagents used to perform diagnostic tests on biological samples outside the body. Includes analyzers, test kits, reagents, and point-of-care devices.
