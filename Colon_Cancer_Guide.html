1 Introduction & Market Opportunity

▾

What Is a Stool DNA Screening Test?

A stool DNA screening test is a non-invasive in-vitro diagnostic (IVD)A medical device or test performed on biological samples (blood, urine, stool) outside the living body — "in glass" — to detect diseases, conditions, or infections. that detects colorectal cancer (CRC)Cancer that begins in the colon or rectum. It is the third most common cancer worldwide and develops through a slow adenoma-to-carcinoma progression over 10–15 years, making it highly suitable for early screening. by analyzing DNA shed from tumour cells into a patient's stool sample. Unlike a colonoscopy, this test requires no bowel preparation, no sedation, and no hospital visit — the patient simply collects a stool sample at home and mails it to a laboratory.

The test works by detecting a panel of biomarkersMeasurable biological molecules (DNA methylation changes, gene mutations, or proteins) whose presence or level indicates a disease state. In CRC screening, multiple biomarkers are combined to improve sensitivity. — including abnormal DNA methylation patterns, cancer-driving gene mutations, and hidden blood — that are characteristic of colorectal tumours and pre-cancerous growths called adenomasBenign (non-cancerous) polyps in the colon lining that can develop into cancer over time. Advanced adenomas (≥1 cm, villous features, or high-grade dysplasia) carry the highest risk of malignant transformation..

Why India Needs This Test

Colorectal cancer is rising rapidly in India. Approximately 57,000 new cases are diagnosed each year, and the incidence is increasing at 3–5% annually — driven by urbanization, dietary shifts toward processed foods, and an aging population. Despite this trend, fewer than 10% of the target population undergoes any form of CRC screening. Most patients present with advanced-stage disease (Stage III/IV), when five-year survival drops below 15%.

3 Biomarker Panel Design

▾

Why Multiple Biomarkers?

No single biomarker can detect all colorectal cancers. Tumours are genetically diverse — some have heavy DNA methylationA chemical modification where a methyl group (CH₃) is added to cytosine bases in DNA, typically at CpG sites. In cancer, certain gene promoters become abnormally hyper-methylated, silencing tumour-suppressor genes. This is detectable by bisulfite conversion + PCR. changes, others carry KRAS mutationsKRAS is a proto-oncogene involved in cell growth signalling. Specific point mutations (like G12D, G12V, G13D) lock the protein in an "always on" state, driving uncontrolled cell proliferation. Found in ~40% of CRCs., and some mainly shed blood. A multitarget panel combining all three categories catches the widest range of tumours.

The Five Biomarkers Explained

1. NDRG4 (N-myc Downstream Regulated Gene 4)

NDRG4A tumour-suppressor gene normally active in the colon. When its promoter becomes hyper-methylated in cancer, the gene is silenced. This methylation event is detectable in stool DNA and is present in 70–90% of CRC cases. is a tumour-suppressor gene that becomes silenced by abnormal methylation in 70–90% of colorectal cancers. It is one of the most sensitive single methylation markers for CRC, with high specificity (>90%) — meaning it rarely triggers false alarms in healthy people. This marker was selected as a core component of Cologuard and has been validated across multiple large clinical trials.

2. BMP3 (Bone Morphogenetic Protein 3)

BMP3A growth-factor gene in the TGF-β signalling pathway. Hyper-methylation of BMP3 disrupts normal cell growth regulation and is found in 60–80% of CRCs and 40–50% of advanced adenomas, making it valuable for detecting pre-cancerous lesions. regulates cell growth through the TGF-β pathway. Its promoter is methylated in 60–80% of CRCs and, critically, in 40–50% of advanced adenomas — making it especially valuable for catching pre-cancerous growths before they become malignant.

3. SDC2 (Syndecan-2)

SDC2A cell-surface proteoglycan gene. Methylation of SDC2 is found in 80–90% of CRCs. It has been validated as a standalone biomarker in Asian populations and forms the backbone of the Chinese ColoClear test. is methylated in 80–90% of CRCs and has been extensively validated in Asian populations. It serves as the backbone of China's ColoClear test and adds detection power for tumours that might be missed by NDRG4 or BMP3 alone. Its inclusion makes the panel particularly suitable for Indian populations.

4. KRAS Mutations

The KRASAn oncogene encoding a signalling protein (GTPase) in the RAS-RAF-MEK-ERK pathway. Point mutations at codons 12 and 13 (e.g., G12D, G12V) permanently activate the protein, driving cancer cell proliferation. Found in ~40% of CRCs and ~25% of advanced adenomas. gene is mutated in approximately 40% of colorectal cancers and 25% of advanced adenomas. The seven most common point mutations at codons 12 and 13 (G12D, G12V, G12C, G12S, G12A, G12R, G13D) are targeted using allele-specific PCR (ARMS)Amplification-Refractory Mutation System — a PCR technique using primers that match perfectly to the mutant sequence but mismatch at the 3' end with wild-type DNA. Only mutant DNA is amplified, enabling detection of mutations present at very low levels (<1% of total DNA).. Since KRAS mutations occur through a different biological mechanism (point mutation vs. methylation), including them captures tumours that methylation markers would miss.

5. Fecal Hemoglobin

Tumours and large adenomas often bleed intermittently. Detecting human hemoglobinThe oxygen-carrying protein in red blood cells. When a tumour bleeds into the intestinal lumen, hemoglobin appears in stool. Unlike DNA markers, hemoglobin detects lesions through a physical mechanism (tissue bleeding), adding an independent detection axis to the panel. in stool via ELISAEnzyme-Linked Immunosorbent Assay — a plate-based technique where antibodies capture a target protein, which is then detected using an enzyme-linked secondary antibody that produces a measurable colour change. Used here to quantify hemoglobin in stool samples. adds a completely independent detection mechanism. Even if a tumour has no methylation changes and no KRAS mutations, it may still bleed — so this protein marker acts as a "safety net" for the panel.

4 The Science: How Detection Works

▾

The Adenoma-Carcinoma Sequence

Colorectal cancer doesn't appear overnight. It follows a slow, stepwise progression called the adenoma-carcinoma sequenceThe biological model describing how normal colon cells accumulate genetic and epigenetic changes over 10–15 years, progressing from normal mucosa → small adenoma → advanced adenoma → invasive carcinoma. Each stage involves different molecular alterations (APC, KRAS, methylation, TP53)., taking 10–15 years on average. Each stage introduces different molecular changes, and our biomarker panel is designed to detect changes at every stage:

Figure 2 — The adenoma-carcinoma sequence: each biomarker class detects changes at different stages, ensuring the panel catches lesions early.

DNA Methylation Detection (qMSP)

The process begins with bisulfite conversionA chemical treatment where sodium bisulfite converts unmethylated cytosine (C) to uracil (U), while methylated cytosine (5mC) remains unchanged. After PCR amplification, the converted U becomes T, creating a sequence difference between methylated and unmethylated DNA that can be detected by methylation-specific primers. — a chemical reaction that distinguishes methylated from unmethylated DNA. Sodium bisulfite converts unmethylated cytosine bases to uracil, while leaving methylated cytosines intact. This creates a detectable sequence difference.

After conversion, quantitative methylation-specific PCR (qMSP)A real-time PCR technique using primers and probes specifically designed to amplify bisulfite-converted, methylated DNA sequences. The primers have cytosines at CpG positions (matching methylated templates) and won't bind efficiently to unmethylated (converted) sequences. TaqMan probes provide fluorescent readout. amplifies only the methylated sequences. TaqMan probes with fluorescent reporters provide real-time quantification. Each methylation marker (NDRG4, BMP3, SDC2) runs in a separate reaction well with its own specific primers and probe.

KRAS Mutation Detection (ARMS-qPCR)

ARMS (Amplification-Refractory Mutation System)A PCR technique where the forward primer's 3' end is designed to perfectly match the mutant base but mismatch the wild-type base. DNA polymerase cannot efficiently extend mismatched 3' ends, so only mutant templates are amplified. Additional engineered mismatches near the 3' end can further improve specificity. exploits a quirk of DNA polymerase: it cannot efficiently extend a primer whose 3' end mismatches the template. By designing primers that match perfectly to each specific KRAS mutation but mismatch normal DNA, only tumour-derived mutant DNA gets amplified — even when it represents less than 1% of the total DNA in the sample.

Hemoglobin Detection (Sandwich ELISA)

The sandwich ELISAA two-antibody immunoassay: a "capture" antibody on the plate surface grabs the target protein; after washing, a second "detection" antibody (linked to an enzyme, usually HRP) binds from the other side, forming a "sandwich." Adding a substrate (TMB) produces a colour change proportional to the target's concentration. uses two antibodies that recognize different parts of the hemoglobin molecule. The capture antibody is coated onto a 96-well microplate. When the stool sample is added, hemoglobin binds to these antibodies. After washing, a second HRP-conjugated antibody binds from the other side. Adding TMB substrate produces a blue colour whose intensity is proportional to the hemoglobin concentration, read by a microplate reader at 450 nm.

Technical Note: The ELISA for hemoglobin uses antibodies specific to human hemoglobin only. This means dietary meat (animal hemoglobin) does not cause false-positive results — unlike the older guaiac-based FOBT test, which cannot distinguish human from animal blood.

6 DNA Extraction Process

▾

Why Stool DNA Extraction Is Challenging

Stool is one of the most difficult biological matrices for DNA extraction. The challenges include: extremely high bacterial DNA background (human DNA is only 0.01–0.1% of total stool DNA), powerful PCR inhibitorsSubstances in stool (bile salts, complex polysaccharides, bilirubin, degradation products) that interfere with DNA polymerase activity during PCR. If not removed during extraction, they can cause false-negative results by preventing DNA amplification. such as bile salts and bilirubin, variable sample consistency, and the need to recover small amounts of tumour-shed DNA from a massive background.

Magnetic Bead-Based Extraction

The recommended approach uses magnetic bead extractionA DNA purification method using silica-coated magnetic particles. DNA binds to the silica surface in high-salt/chaotropic conditions. A magnetic plate holds the beads while wash buffers remove contaminants. Finally, a low-salt elution buffer releases purified DNA. Fully automatable using instruments like KingFisher Apex. on a KingFisher ApexAn automated magnetic particle processor by Thermo Fisher Scientific. It uses magnetic rods with disposable tip combs to move magnetic beads through a series of wash and elution steps in a 96-well deep-well plate. Processes 96 samples simultaneously in about 30 minutes. instrument, which processes 96 samples simultaneously in approximately 30 minutes.

Protocol Steps

1
Homogenize: Take a 2–4 mL aliquot of the stool-buffer mixture. Vortex vigorously for 60 seconds to ensure uniform distribution of DNA.
2
Clarify: Centrifuge at 5,000 × g for 10 minutes. Transfer the clear supernatant (containing dissolved DNA) to a new tube, leaving behind solid debris.
3
Bind: Add silica-coated magnetic beads and binding buffer (high-salt, chaotropic conditions). DNA adsorbs to the silica surface. Incubate for 10 minutes with gentle mixing.
4
Wash (×3): Using the KingFisher's magnetic rod, transfer beads (with DNA) through three sequential wash wells — Wash 1 (guanidinium-based), Wash 2 (70% ethanol), Wash 3 (70% ethanol). Each wash removes PCR inhibitors and contaminants.
5
Dry: Brief air-dry step (2–3 min) to evaporate residual ethanol, which would inhibit downstream PCR.
6
Elute: Transfer beads to elution buffer (10 mM Tris-HCl, pH 8.0, 50–100 μL). At low salt, DNA releases from the silica beads into solution. This eluate is the purified DNA for PCR.

7 PCR & Detection Protocols

▾

Overview of the Detection Workflow

Figure 3 — Complete testing workflow from sample receipt through extraction, bisulfite conversion, parallel detection assays, and combined result reporting.

A. Bisulfite Conversion Protocol

This is the essential first step for methylation detection. Sodium bisulfite treatment converts unmethylated cytosines to uracil, while methylated cytosines remain protected. Approximately 500 ng–1 μg of extracted DNA is treated with bisulfite reagent, incubated at 95°C (denaturation) and 60°C (conversion) in thermal cycling steps over ~4 hours, then cleaned up using silica columns. Expected recovery is 60–80% of input DNA.

B. Methylation qMSP Protocol

Three separate reactions per sample, one for each methylation marker:

ComponentVolumeNotes
2× TaqMan Master Mix10 μLHot-start polymerase, dNTPs, buffer
Forward primer (10 μM)0.8 μLMethylation-specific sequence
Reverse primer (10 μM)0.8 μLMethylation-specific sequence
TaqMan probe (5 μM)0.4 μLFAM/BHQ1 labelled
Bisulfite-converted DNA5 μL~25–50 ng equivalent
Nuclease-free water3 μLTo 20 μL total volume

Cycling conditions: 95°C × 10 min (activation) → 45 cycles of [95°C × 15 s, 60°C × 60 s (read fluorescence)].

Interpretation: A Ct value below the validated cutoff (typically 35–38) for any methylation marker = positive for that marker.

C. KRAS ARMS-qPCR Protocol

Seven separate reactions per sample, one for each KRAS mutation (G12D, G12V, G12C, G12S, G12A, G12R, G13D) plus a wild-type control. Each reaction uses allele-specific forward primers with an engineered mismatch at position −2 from the 3' end to enhance specificity. Total reaction volume: 20 μL with similar composition to qMSP but using mutation-specific primers and unconverted (genomic) DNA.

Cycling conditions: 95°C × 10 min → 45 cycles of [95°C × 15 s, 62°C × 60 s (read)].

Interpretation: ΔCt (mutant Ct − wild-type Ct) below the validated threshold (typically ≤8) = positive for that KRAS mutation.

D. Hemoglobin Sandwich ELISA Protocol

1
Coat Plate: Add anti-human hemoglobin capture antibody (2 μg/mL in carbonate buffer, pH 9.6) to 96-well microplate. Incubate overnight at 4°C.
2
Block: Add 1% BSA blocking buffer. Incubate 1 hour at 37°C. Wash ×3 with PBS-Tween.
3
Add Samples: Add stool supernatant (diluted 1:10–1:100). Include hemoglobin standards (0–500 ng/mL). Incubate 2 hours at 37°C.
4
Detection Antibody: Wash ×3. Add HRP-conjugated anti-hemoglobin antibody. Incubate 1 hour at 37°C.
5
Develop: Wash ×5. Add TMB substrate. Incubate 15–20 min in the dark (blue colour develops).
6
Read: Add stop solution (2N H₂SO₄ — turns yellow). Read absorbance at 450 nm. Hemoglobin ≥50 ng/mL = positive.

Hub-and-Spoke Model for India

Given India's size and logistics challenges, the recommended model is a hub-and-spokeA centralized processing model where samples from multiple collection points ("spokes" — clinics, hospitals, pharmacies) are shipped to regional processing laboratories ("hubs") for analysis. This concentrates expensive equipment and trained personnel while keeping collection accessible. network: collection kits are distributed through clinics, hospitals, and pharmacies (spokes) across a region, while samples are shipped to centralized processing laboratories (hubs) in major cities. Starting with 3–4 hubs (e.g., Mumbai, Delhi, Bengaluru, Kolkata) can cover most of India's urban and semi-urban population.

CDSCO Regulatory Pathway (India)

In India, IVD devices are regulated by the CDSCOCentral Drugs Standard Control Organisation — India's national regulatory authority for pharmaceuticals, medical devices, and IVDs. It operates under the Medical Device Rules 2017 and classifies IVDs into Classes A–D based on risk, with Class D being the highest risk category. under the Medical Device Rules 2017. A stool DNA test for cancer screening is classified as Class C or Class D (high-risk IVD) and requires clinical validation before approval.

Regulatory Timeline (18–30 months)

1
Pre-submission (Months 1–6): Establish ISO 13485 QMS. Prepare technical documentation — design history file, risk management file, performance evaluation plan. Apply for Form 28 (manufacturing licence).
2
Clinical Investigation (Months 6–18): Apply for Form 44 (clinical investigation licence). Conduct clinical validation study with 500–1,000 subjects across 3–5 sites. Compare against colonoscopy as reference standard. Demonstrate required sensitivity and specificity.
3
Submission & Review (Months 18–24): Submit complete technical dossier to CDSCO, including clinical data, analytical performance data, stability data, and manufacturing documentation. CDSCO review typically takes 6–12 months.
4
Approval & Launch (Months 24–30): Receive marketing authorization. Begin commercial manufacturing. Initiate post-market surveillance as required.

Searchable Glossary

Adenoma
A benign polyp in the colon lining that can develop into cancer over time. Advanced adenomas (≥1 cm, villous features, or high-grade dysplasia) carry the highest risk.
ARMS (Amplification-Refractory Mutation System)
A PCR technique using primers designed to perfectly match a specific mutation at the 3' end, enabling selective amplification of mutant DNA even when vastly outnumbered by normal DNA.
Biomarker
A measurable biological molecule (DNA methylation, gene mutation, or protein) whose presence or level indicates a disease state.
Bisulfite Conversion
Chemical treatment with sodium bisulfite that converts unmethylated cytosine to uracil while leaving methylated cytosine unchanged, enabling PCR-based methylation detection.
BMP3 (Bone Morphogenetic Protein 3)
A growth-factor gene whose promoter becomes hyper-methylated in 60–80% of CRCs and 40–50% of advanced adenomas.
CDSCO
Central Drugs Standard Control Organisation — India's regulatory authority for pharmaceuticals, medical devices, and IVDs under the Medical Device Rules 2017.
COGS (Cost of Goods Sold)
The direct costs attributable to producing each test, including reagents, consumables, collection kit, shipping, and direct labour.
Cologuard
The first FDA-approved multi-target stool DNA test (Exact Sciences, 2014). Combines NDRG4, BMP3, KRAS, β-actin, and hemoglobin markers. The benchmark product in this category.
CRC (Colorectal Cancer)
Cancer of the colon or rectum — the third most common cancer worldwide. Develops through the adenoma-carcinoma sequence over 10–15 years.
Ct (Cycle Threshold)
In real-time PCR, the cycle number at which fluorescence crosses a set threshold. Lower Ct = more starting target DNA. Used to determine positive/negative results.
DTT (Dithiothreitol)
A reducing agent that breaks disulphide bonds in proteins. Used in the stabilization buffer to denature nucleases and proteases.
EDTA
Ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid — a chelating agent that binds divalent metal ions (Mg²⁺, Ca²⁺) required by DNA-degrading nucleases.
ELISA (Enzyme-Linked Immunosorbent Assay)
A plate-based immunoassay using antibodies to capture and detect a target protein. In this test, sandwich ELISA detects human hemoglobin in stool.
FIT (Fecal Immunochemical Test)
A stool test that uses antibodies to detect human hemoglobin. Simpler and cheaper than stool DNA testing but with lower sensitivity for CRC (74%) and much lower adenoma detection (24%).
gFOBT (Guaiac Fecal Occult Blood Test)
An older stool blood test using a chemical reaction (guaiac) to detect hemoglobin. Cannot distinguish human from animal blood and has low sensitivity (50–60%).
GuSCN (Guanidinium Thiocyanate)
A powerful chaotropic salt that denatures proteins and disrupts cell membranes. The primary DNA-preserving agent in the stool stabilization buffer (used at 5 M concentration).
HRP (Horseradish Peroxidase)
An enzyme conjugated to the detection antibody in ELISA. It catalyses the oxidation of TMB substrate to produce a measurable colour change.
Hub-and-Spoke Model
A logistics model where samples from distributed collection points (spokes) are shipped to centralized processing laboratories (hubs) — concentrating expensive equipment while keeping collection accessible.
ISO 13485
International quality management standard for medical device and IVD manufacturers. Required by CDSCO for Indian manufacturing licences.
IVD (In-Vitro Diagnostic)
A medical test performed on biological samples outside the living body to detect diseases, conditions, or infections.
KingFisher Apex
An automated magnetic particle processor (Thermo Fisher) that uses magnetic rods to move beads through 96-well plates for DNA extraction. Processes 96 samples in ~30 minutes.
KRAS
An oncogene encoding a GTPase signalling protein. Point mutations at codons 12 and 13 drive uncontrolled cell proliferation and are found in ~40% of CRCs.
LIMS (Laboratory Information Management System)
Software that tracks samples, results, quality data, and reagent lots through the entire laboratory workflow from receipt to reporting.
Methylation
Addition of a methyl group (CH₃) to DNA, typically at CpG sites. Abnormal hyper-methylation of tumour-suppressor gene promoters silences their expression and is a hallmark of cancer.
NDRG4 (N-myc Downstream Regulated Gene 4)
A tumour-suppressor gene silenced by methylation in 70–90% of CRCs. One of the most sensitive single methylation markers for colorectal cancer.
PCR (Polymerase Chain Reaction)
A technique that amplifies specific DNA sequences exponentially through repeated cycles of denaturation, annealing, and extension. Real-time (quantitative) PCR monitors amplification in real time using fluorescent probes.
qMSP (Quantitative Methylation-Specific PCR)
A real-time PCR method using primers and probes designed to specifically amplify methylated (bisulfite-converted) DNA sequences. Combines methylation specificity with quantitative readout.
SDC2 (Syndecan-2)
A cell-surface proteoglycan gene methylated in 80–90% of CRCs. Extensively validated in Asian populations and the backbone of China's ColoClear test.
SDS (Sodium Dodecyl Sulphate)
An anionic detergent that disrupts cell membranes and denatures proteins. Used in the stabilization buffer to lyse cells and release DNA.
Sensitivity
The proportion of true positives correctly identified by the test (true positives / [true positives + false negatives]). Higher sensitivity means fewer missed cancers.
Specificity
The proportion of true negatives correctly identified (true negatives / [true negatives + false positives]). Higher specificity means fewer false alarms.
TaqMan Probe
A fluorescently labelled oligonucleotide probe used in real-time PCR. It binds between the two primers and is cleaved by the polymerase during amplification, releasing a fluorescent signal proportional to the amount of target DNA.
TMB (3,3',5,5'-Tetramethylbenzidine)
A chromogenic substrate for HRP in ELISA. Turns blue when oxidised by HRP, then yellow when the reaction is stopped with acid. Absorbance at 450 nm is proportional to target concentration.
