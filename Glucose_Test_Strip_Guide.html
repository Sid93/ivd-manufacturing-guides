1 Introduction & Market Overview

▾

What Are Glucose Test Strips?

Glucose test strips are small, disposable electrochemical biosensorsA device that converts a biological reaction (here, glucose oxidation by an enzyme) into a measurable electrical signal — typically a current proportional to glucose concentration. that allow diabetic patients to measure their blood glucose levels at home. A tiny drop of blood (typically 0.3–1.0 μL) is applied to the strip, which is inserted into a handheld meter. Within seconds the meter displays a glucose reading.

The technology traces back to 1962 when Clark and Lyons described the first enzyme electrode. Modern strips integrate multiple functional layers — substrate, electrodes, reagent, adhesive, and membrane — into a compact device manufactured by high-speed screen printingA thick-film printing technique where ink is forced through a patterned mesh screen onto a substrate using a squeegee. It deposits films 10–50 μm thick and is the dominant method for commercial glucose strip production. technology.

How Does It Work?

When blood contacts the reagent layer, an enzymeA biological catalyst — a protein that speeds up a specific chemical reaction. In glucose strips, the enzyme selectively reacts with glucose and ignores other sugars. (usually glucose oxidase or glucose dehydrogenase) selectively oxidises glucose. Electrons released during this reaction are transferred via a redox mediatorA small molecule that shuttles electrons from the enzyme's active site to the electrode surface. Common mediators include ferricyanide and ferrocene. Without a mediator, the enzyme cannot efficiently communicate with the electrode. (such as ferricyanide) to a working electrodeThe electrode where the electrochemical reaction of interest occurs. In glucose strips, it collects electrons from the mediator and the resulting current is proportional to glucose concentration.. The meter applies a voltage and measures the resulting current, which is proportional to the glucose concentration.

Why In-House Manufacturing? Controlling your own strip production allows you to customise formulations, protect intellectual property, reduce supply-chain dependency, and potentially achieve significant cost savings at scale. Capital investment ranges from $300K for pilot production to $10M+ for a full commercial line.

2 Five-Layer Strip Architecture

▾

Every glucose test strip is built from five functional layers stacked on top of each other. Each layer has a specific job, and the materials plus fabrication method for each layer critically affect performance.

Cross-section of a typical electrochemical glucose test strip showing all five functional layers

Layer 1: Substrate

The substrate is the bottom-most layer providing mechanical support. PET (Polyethylene Terephthalate) is the most widely used material — it offers excellent dimensional stability, chemical resistance, and compatibility with screen printing. PET films are typically 0.25–0.4 mm thick and provide a smooth, non-porous surface for electrode deposition.

Alternatives include polyester films (suitable for roll-to-rollA continuous manufacturing process where a flexible substrate (like PET film) is unwound from a roll, processed through various stations (printing, drying, lamination), and rewound — enabling very high throughput. manufacturing), paper substrates (ultra-low-cost, capillary-driven), and FR4 boards with gold plating for premium strips.

Layer 2: Electrodes

The electrode layer comprises working, counter, and reference electrodes screen-printed onto the substrate. Carbon-based inks are the most common working electrode material — low cost, wide potential window, and compatible with enzyme immobilisation. Ag/AgCl (silver/silver chloride) provides a stable reference potential. Silver conductive inks form the traces connecting electrodes to contact pads.

Layer 3: Reagent (Most Critical)

This layer contains the enzyme, mediator, and polymer matrix. It is the heart of the sensor and determines sensitivity, linearity, and shelf life. The validated GOx-ferricyanide formulation is the industry standard (detailed in Section 5).

Layer 4: Adhesive & Spacer

Pressure-sensitive adhesive (PSA)An adhesive that bonds when pressure is applied, without needing heat, water, or solvent. Double-sided PSA tapes are die-cut with a gap that forms the capillary channel in the strip. tapes serve double duty: they seal the strip and their die-cut gap defines the capillary channel through which blood flows to the electrodes. The spacer thickness (typically 50–100 μm) directly controls sample volume.

Layer 5: Membrane / Cover

The top cover controls the precise blood volume (0.3–1.0 μL) that contacts the electrode, limits diffusion to extend the linear range, and can exclude interferents like ascorbic acid and uric acid. Common materials include cellulose and polycarbonate membranes.

3 Electrode Ink Formulations

▾

Screen-printable inks are the foundation of high-volume glucose strip manufacturing. Ink formulations must balance rheological propertiesThe flow and deformation characteristics of a material — viscosity (how thick it is), thixotropy (how it thins when stirred), and yield stress. These determine how well ink transfers through the screen mesh. (viscosity, thixotropy), electrical performance (conductivity, electrochemical activity), and processing requirements (drying time, curing temperature).

Carbon Inks (Working Electrode)

Carbon inks consist of conductive carbon particles dispersed in a polymer binder with solvent. They come in two main types:

Enhanced Carbon Inks

Carbon inks can be modified with electrocatalystsMaterials that lower the energy barrier for an electrochemical reaction. Adding electrocatalysts to carbon ink means you can detect glucose at lower voltages, reducing interference from other blood components. or nanoparticles to improve performance:

  • Iridium nanoparticles — enable low-potential H₂O₂ detection
  • Cupric-hexacyanoferrate — operates at -0.1V vs Ag/AgCl, eliminating anti-interference membranes
  • Palladium particles — decreases overvoltage for H₂O₂ oxidation by >0.5V
  • MWCNTs (Multi-Walled Carbon Nanotubes) — enhance electron transfer and increase surface area
  • Prussian blue — selective H₂O₂ detection at low potentials
  • Graphene inks — superior conductivity, compatible with stencil printing

Silver & Ag/AgCl Inks (Reference Electrode)

Silver inks provide high electrical conductivity for traces and counter electrodes. The reference electrode uses Ag/AgCl, applied either as pre-mixed commercial ink or by printing silver paste followed by chloride treatment.

Insulator Inks

Insulator inks define the exposed electrode area, prevent electrical shorting, and create channel walls. Options include UV-curable inks (rapid curing, excellent precision) and PVC-based plastisol inks (cure at 149–166°C).

Emerging: PEDOT:PSSPoly(3,4-ethylenedioxythiophene):poly(styrenesulfonate) — a conductive polymer used for flexible, transparent electrodes. Optimal formulations contain 5% ethylene glycol for improved electrical properties. conductive polymer inks enable flexible, transparent electrodes for wearable glucose sensors. Polyaniline (PANi) nanoparticle inks can be inkjet-printed for next-generation biosensor applications.

Polymer Matrices & Binders

The enzyme and mediator are held in place by a polymer matrix that must allow glucose to diffuse in while stabilising the enzyme:

  • CMC (Carboxymethylcellulose) — 10 mg/mL; viscosity modifier and enzyme stabiliser
  • Alginate — 25 mg/mL; forms a gel matrix for immobilisation
  • Chitosan — biocompatible; used in inkjet-printed sensors
  • Nafion — perfluorinated membrane; excludes interferents, entraps enzyme
  • Sol-gel silica — excellent long-term enzyme stability

5 Reagent Layer Formulations

▾

The reagent layer formulation critically determines sensor performance, stability, and shelf life. Here are the key validated recipes.

The Industry-Standard GOx-Ferricyanide Formulation

Validated Recipe (Zolotukhina et al.): This is the most widely replicated and validated formulation for screen-printed glucose strips. It provides excellent sensitivity and shelf life.
ComponentConcentrationFunction
Glucose oxidase (GOx)24 mg/mLEnzyme catalyst — selectively oxidises glucose

Citrate bufferpH 6Maintains optimal pH for enzyme activity

This formulation is applied by screen printing or jet dispensing onto carbon working electrodes. Drying conditions and reagent volume are critical for performance.

Alternative Formulations

GOx-Ferrocene System (Inkjet-Printable)

  • Glucose oxidase + ferrocene complex as mediator + chitosan binder
  • Operating potential: 0.25V vs. PEDOT:PSS reference electrode
  • Detection range: 28 μM to 0.85 mM (suitable for saliva glucose)
  • Fully inkjet-printable on paper substrates

FAD-GDH-Ferricyanide System (Oxygen-Independent)

  • FAD-GDH enzyme + ferricyanide mediator on palladium electrodes
  • Operating potential: 0.3V; sample volume: only 0.4 μL
  • Eliminates oxygen interference — important for venous blood samples

Non-Enzymatic Approach (Enzyme-Free)

  • Copper oxide (CuO) electrodes with cellulose microfluidic pH boosting
  • No enzyme or mediator needed — simplified manufacturing
  • Better long-term stability since no protein degradation
  • Still experimental; lower specificity for glucose
Critical Drying Parameters: After reagent deposition, drying at 30–40°C with controlled humidity preserves enzyme activity. Over-drying can denature enzymes (reducing sensitivity); under-drying shortens shelf life. Most commercial strips achieve 18–24 month shelf life with optimised drying protocols.

Key Process Details

Electrode Printing (Step 2)

Screen printing deposits thick films (10–50 μm) with excellent reproducibility. A fine mesh screen coated with photosensitive emulsion is exposed through a photomask to create the electrode pattern. Ink is forced through openings using a squeegeeA flexible blade (usually polyurethane) that sweeps across the screen surface, pushing ink through the open mesh areas onto the substrate below.. Multiple passes deposit working, reference, counter electrodes, and insulator layers — each layer requires drying before the next.

Reagent Deposition (Step 5)

This is the most critical step. Precision jet dispensers (such as the Nordson ASYMTEK DJ-9000) deposit controlled volumes of enzyme solution. Typical dispensed volume is about 0.45 mg GOx onto the reaction zone. Drop-on-demand systems with volume feedback and vision alignment ensure precise placement.

Cost Reduction Strategies

  • Use standard thermal-cure inks (UV inks cost up to 3x more)
  • Water-based formulations are generally cheaper than organic-solvent inks
  • Non-enzymatic sensors eliminate enzyme costs entirely (but are still experimental)
  • Paper substrates offer ultra-low-cost alternatives for resource-limited settings
  • Wax screen printing eliminates need for $2,500+ wax printers (screens cost <$5)

10 Quality Assurance & Regulatory

▾

Quality Control Methods

Electrical Testing

Automated testers check every strip for resistance and electrode integrity using the 4-wire Kelvin methodA precision resistance measurement technique that uses four wires — two to carry current and two to measure voltage — eliminating the resistance of the connecting wires from the measurement. Essential for measuring low electrode resistances accurately.. Equipment: NI PXI-4130 Source Measure Unit with automated test fixtures.

Electrochemical Testing

Functional testing verifies glucose response using potentiostats (Gamry, Metrohm Autolab) with standard glucose solutions across the clinical range (20–600 mg/dL). Cyclic voltammetry and chronoamperometry verify electrode behaviour and calibration.

Visual Inspection

CCD camera systems with image-processing software detect print defects, misalignment, reagent coverage issues, and contamination. Automated rejection systems remove defective strips from the production line.

Reference Testing

Glucose readings from manufactured strips are verified against the YSI Stat 2300 — the gold-standard laboratory glucose analyser. Statistical comparison ensures accuracy meets regulatory requirements.

11 Future Directions & Glossary

▾

Emerging Technologies

Advanced Materials

  • Laser-engraved graphene (LEG) — CO₂ laser scribing of polyimide films creates porous graphene electrodes without inks. Decorated with copper nanocubes for enhanced sensitivity.
  • Carbon nanotubes & metal nanoparticles — enhance sensitivity and reduce operating potentials
  • Conductive polymers (PEDOT:PSS, polyaniline) — enable flexible, wearable glucose sensors

Alternative Fabrication

  • Fully inkjet-printed sensors — all-printed systems on paper for ultra-low cost
  • 3D printing — rapid prototyping of customised sensor geometries
  • Roll-to-roll flexographic printing — fast commercial production with 500 μm microfluidic channels

Next-Generation Approaches

  • Non-enzymatic sensors — CuO or noble metal electrodes eliminate enzyme stability concerns
  • Wearable/continuous monitoring — flexible substrates, sweat glucose analysis, wireless communication
  • Multi-analyte strips — twin-enzyme strips detecting glucose + lactate or other metabolites; 4-in-1 strips in development
  • Paper-based microfluidics — capillary-driven flow eliminates pumps; suitable for resource-limited settings

Searchable Glossary

Amperometry
Electrochemical technique where a constant potential is applied and the resulting current is measured. The current is proportional to the analyte concentration.
Biosensor
An analytical device that combines a biological recognition element (enzyme, antibody) with a transducer to produce a measurable signal proportional to the target analyte concentration.
Capillary Channel
A narrow gap (50–100 μm) formed between the adhesive spacer layer and the cover membrane. Surface tension draws the blood sample into the channel and onto the electrode by capillary action.
Chronoamperometry
A technique where a voltage step is applied and current is measured as a function of time. Used to characterise and calibrate glucose sensors.
CMC (Carboxymethylcellulose)
A water-soluble cellulose derivative used as a viscosity modifier and enzyme stabiliser in the reagent layer at typical concentrations of 10 mg/mL.
Counter Electrode
Completes the electrochemical circuit. Current flows between the working electrode and counter electrode. Often made of carbon or silver ink.
Cyclic Voltammetry (CV)
An electrochemical technique where the potential is swept linearly back and forth between two values while current is measured. Used to characterise electrode reactions and mediator behaviour.
Electrochemical Biosensor
A biosensor where the biological reaction produces an electrical signal (current, voltage, or impedance change) that is measured by electrodes. Glucose test strips are amperometric electrochemical biosensors.
FAD-GDH
Flavin Adenine Dinucleotide-Glucose Dehydrogenase. An oxygen-independent enzyme alternative to GOx that eliminates oxygen interference in glucose measurements.
Ferricyanide
Potassium ferricyanide K₃[Fe(CN)₆] — the most widely used redox mediator in commercial glucose strips. Used at ~200 mg/mL concentration.
Ferrocene
An organometallic compound with stable, reversible redox behaviour at low potentials. Used as a mediator either dissolved in the reagent or covalently bound to the enzyme.
GOx (Glucose Oxidase)
An enzyme (from Aspergillus niger) that selectively catalyses the oxidation of glucose to gluconolactone. The most widely used enzyme in glucose biosensors. Typical concentration: 24 mg/mL.
ISO 15197
International standard specifying requirements for blood glucose monitoring systems used by patients for self-testing. Defines accuracy criteria (±15 mg/dL or ±15%) for regulatory compliance.
Lamination
The process of bonding layers together using pressure-sensitive adhesive (PSA) tapes or heat. Creates the sealed strip structure with defined capillary channels.
Mediator (Redox)
A small molecule that shuttles electrons between the enzyme active site and the electrode surface. Enables detection at lower potentials than direct H₂O₂ oxidation. Examples: ferricyanide, ferrocene, osmium complexes.
MWCNT
Multi-Walled Carbon Nanotubes — cylindrical nanostructures of carbon with multiple concentric walls. Added to inks to enhance conductivity and electrochemical surface area.
Nafion
A perfluorinated sulfonated polymer membrane (by DuPont) that allows cations to pass but blocks anions and large molecules. Used to exclude interferents like ascorbic acid from the electrode surface.
Overpotential
The extra voltage beyond the thermodynamic minimum needed to drive an electrochemical reaction at a useful rate. Electrocatalysts and mediators reduce overpotential.
PET (Polyethylene Terephthalate)
The most common substrate material for glucose strips — a thermoplastic polymer with excellent dimensional stability, chemical resistance, and compatibility with screen printing. Thickness: 0.25–0.4 mm.
Potentiostat
An electronic instrument that controls the voltage between working and reference electrodes while measuring the current. Essential for electrochemical testing and QC of glucose strips.
PSA (Pressure-Sensitive Adhesive)
An adhesive that bonds on contact with light pressure, no heat or solvent needed. Die-cut PSA tapes define the capillary channel geometry in glucose strips.
Prussian Blue
An inorganic pigment (iron hexacyanoferrate) used as an electrocatalyst for selective H₂O₂ detection at low potentials, reducing interference from ascorbic acid and uric acid.
Reference Electrode
An electrode with a stable, known potential against which other electrode potentials are measured. In glucose strips, Ag/AgCl (silver/silver chloride) is the standard reference material.
Roll-to-Roll (R2R)
A continuous manufacturing process where flexible substrate is unwound from a roll, processed through printing/coating/cutting stations, and rewound. Enables very high throughput (>10⁶ strips/hour).
Screen Printing
A thick-film printing technique where ink is forced through a patterned mesh screen using a squeegee. Deposits 10–50 μm films with excellent reproducibility. Dominates commercial glucose strip production (~50% of market).
Squeegee
A flexible blade (typically polyurethane) used in screen printing to sweep ink across the screen, forcing it through open mesh areas onto the substrate below.
Thick Film
A film deposited by screen printing, typically 10–50 μm thick. Contrasted with thin films (<1 μm) made by vapour deposition or sputtering.
Working Electrode
The electrode where the electrochemical reaction of interest occurs. In glucose strips, it is typically made of carbon ink and is where the mediator transfers electrons, generating the measured current.
YSI Stat 2300
A laboratory glucose analyser by Yellow Springs Instruments — the gold-standard reference instrument for verifying glucose strip accuracy during quality control testing.
